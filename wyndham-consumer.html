Wyndham Consumer

Designed the consumer portal experience for the world's largest vacation timeshare company, with processes ranging from exploring resorts and booking vacations to learning about product offerings and managing membership accounts.

Client
  • Wyndham Vacation Ownership (WVO)
Deliverables
  • Wireframes
  • Site Maps
  • Work Flows/User Flows
  • Interaction Models

Problem

Timeshare owners at Wyndham Vacation Ownership (WVO) are mostly older, retired folk who use the online portal to search and book vacations, utilize points, and manage accounts, among many other tasks. The existing consumer portal is outdated and often leaves users to resort to its customer service line for support requests and inquiries.

Thus, the client's ask was a complete optimization of WVO's consumer portal to produce an organized, educational, and effortless experience.


Process

The team consisted of myself, another UX designer, our VP of UX, an account supervisor, a producer, a business analyst, two visual designers, and two front-end developers. For each major section of the site, we underwent a two-week-long design sprint. Each week, we had 2-3 internal meetings and 1-2 client calls during which we strategized, brainstormed, and/or critiqued designs to ensure that we were aligned with the client's requirements and timeline.

My tasks included:

  • Problem Exploration
    Analyzing functional requirements, all sorts of esoteric business rules, and high-level user needs.

  • Information Architecture
    Organizing data and requirements into spreadsheets/matrices. Developing sitemaps and userflows.

  • Competitive Analysis
    Examining best practices and common design patterns for particular features (e.g., results listings, filtering, sorting, map views, forms).

  • Interaction Design
    Mapping out interaction models, process flows, and error studies. Translating IA into low-fidelity wireframes.

  • Functional Specifications
    Documenting technical and business requirements for page elements and the user interaction.

  • Client Presentation
    Structuring, creating, and annotating presentation decks.

"No dead ends when searching for a vacation."

WVO's philosophy is that the customer should rarely reach a dead end while searching for a vacation. Achieving this was challenging considering that WVO's program-wide rules, resort-specific rules, and even the owner's membership type all come into play to determine whether or not a specific resort and unit can be booked for a selected date range.

For example, a date range selection may be valid for one resort but violates a minimum length of stay requirement for another resort. But since we don't want to block the customer from viewing that invalid resort, we configured the date selection logic to allow a Dateless Search--providing the user flexibility with dates, locations, and room types.

Given the complexity of these booking requirements, I sifted through WVO resorts' rules and deduced three possible date-related error scenarios:

  1. User selects a check-in date that violates resort's check-in day of week rule.
  2. User selects a check-out date that violates resort's check-out day of week rule.
  3. User selects a check-out date that does not meet resort's minimum/maximum length of stay rule.

These scenarios were used to form the framework to cover the bases for error handling, covered in a bit below.


"How can we intuitively display the availability for all the different room types?"

Initially, our team explored the option to view availability for all room types at a single resort at the same time. The user could scan a resort's calendar in order to determine their desired room on the desired date range. We ultimately concluded that this treatment would display too much information and confuse the user since it's not quite representative of the standard monthly calendar.

All Rooms At Once

Subsequently, we proceeded with allowing the user to toggle through and view available dates for one room type at a time.

One Room At A Time

Shown here is an error state that captures the check-in day rule at this particular resort. I initially wanted to take a preventative approach and display this selected date (Sept. 16) as unavailable from the start. On hover, a message could indicate why exactly the room type is unavailable on this date. However, we were told that WVO's CMS wouldn't be able to display multiple error messages if the case arises, and so we adopted a responsive erroring approach.

Responsive Erroring

But on selection of a check-in date, the system can narrow down booking restrictions considerably enough so as to load in the resort's maximum length of stay rule, as shown.

Maximum Length Of Stay

On selection of valid check-in and check-out dates, the user can proceed to book the room, or if desired, run a new search with these dates. I felt that including the latter option could prove useful, since users may only be determining their dates of travel in the process of using the availability calendar. If this happens to be the case, the user may want to view other resorts with the selected date range in mind.

Valid Dates Selected

Shown here is a state to view information about a specific room type. Initially, I designed it to load in place of the calendar so the user can toggle between and compare various room types. But since the nature of the information is auxiliary to the core experience of selecting dates, I modified the state to exist in an overlay.

Lessons Learned

The biggest challenge of all was striving to create a simple experience while incorporating WVO's esoteric business rules. This required constant communication with the client's product owners and developers to ensure that we had a full grasp on the product requirements and technical feasibility.

While some of the travel industry's beasts such as Kayak and AirBnB provided good insight into smart design patterns, a crucial lesson I learned is that best practices falter if the design does not align with the product's business model, especially given WVO's unique program rules and user needs.

Explore Resorts

"Explore what WVO has to offer"

The Explore Resorts experience allows owners to discover and learn about WVO's various resort offerings. Features include: search with filters, list + map results, and a compare tool.

This landing page acts as the entry point for users to search for resorts. Here, we designed the search widget to pre-filter resorts by Location, Vacation Experience (e.g., Beaches, Mountains, Metropolitan), and/or Special Needs. Additionally, the area below the search widget showcases thematic groupings of resorts for inspirational browsing.

Landing Page

Given that this feature is intended more for exploring than it is for finding a specific resort, we figured that users would want to scout out and plan potential stays based on location (e.g., resort near Disney World). Thus, we opted for a dual list and map experience to encourage users to explore resorts visually--a la AirBnB, Yelp, Foursquare, etc.
